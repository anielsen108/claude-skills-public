# Rhetorical Devices Reference

Comprehensive reference for device identification and glossary composition. Consult when encountering unfamiliar devices or composing detailed glossary entries.

## Classical Figures (Greek & Latin)

### Repetition Figures

**anaphora** /əˈnæfərə/ (Greek ἀναφορά, "carrying back")
Repetition at the beginning of successive clauses or sentences.
"Mad world! Mad kings! Mad composition!"

**epistrophe** /ɪˈpɪstrəfi/ (Greek ἐπιστροφή, "turning upon")
Repetition at the end of successive clauses. Also: epiphora.
"...of the people, by the people, for the people"

**symploce** /ˈsɪmpləsi/ (Greek συμπλοκή, "interweaving")
Combination of anaphora and epistrophe.
"When there is talk of hatred, let us stand up and talk against it. When there is talk of violence, let us stand up and talk against it."

**anadiplosis** /ˌænədɪˈploʊsɪs/ (Greek ἀναδίπλωσις, "doubling back")
The end of one clause repeated at the start of the next.
"Fear leads to anger. Anger leads to hate."

**epanalepsis** /ˌɛpənəˈlɛpsɪs/ (Greek ἐπανάληψις, "resumption")
Beginning of a clause or sentence repeated at its end.
"The king is dead, long live the king."

**polyptoton** /ˌpɒlɪpˈtoʊtɒn/ (Greek πολύπτωτον, "many cases")
Repetition of words from the same root in different grammatical forms.
"Love is not love which alters when it alteration finds."

**antimetabole** /ˌæntɪmɛˈtæbəli/ (Greek ἀντιμεταβολή, "turning about")
Repetition of words in reverse grammatical order.
"Ask not what your country can do for you; ask what you can do for your country."

### Structural Figures

**chiasmus** /kaɪˈæzməs/ (Greek χιασμός, from χ "chi")
ABBA pattern; reversal of grammatical structures without necessarily repeating words.
"By day the frolic, and the dance by night."

**antithesis** /ænˈtɪθəsɪs/ (Greek ἀντίθεσις, "opposition")
Juxtaposition of contrasting ideas in balanced phrases.
"It was the best of times, it was the worst of times."

**isocolon** /aɪˈsɒkələn/ (Greek ἰσόκωλον, "equal member")
Parallel structure with clauses of equal length and corresponding structure.
"Veni, vidi, vici." ("I came, I saw, I conquered.")

**tricolon** /traɪˈkoʊlɒn/ (Greek τρία + κῶλον, "three members")
Series of three parallel elements, often with increasing force.
"Government of the people, by the people, for the people."

**climax/auxesis** /ˈklaɪmæks/ /ɔːkˈsiːsɪs/ (Greek κλῖμαξ "ladder," αὔξησις "growth")
Arrangement in ascending order of importance or intensity.
"I came, I saw, I conquered."

**anticlimax/bathos** /ˌæntɪˈklaɪmæks/ /ˈbeɪθɒs/ (Greek βάθος, "depth")
Descending order, often creating ironic or comic effect.
"For God, for country, and for Yale."

### Comparison & Substitution

**metaphor** /ˈmɛtəfɔːr/ (Greek μεταφορά, "transference")
Implicit comparison; one thing is said to be another.
"All the world's a stage."

**simile** /ˈsɪmɪli/ (Latin similis, "like")
Explicit comparison using "like" or "as."
"My love is like a red, red rose."

**metonymy** /mɛˈtɒnɪmi/ (Greek μετωνυμία, "change of name")
Substitution by association or contiguity.
"The pen is mightier than the sword." (writing/warfare)

**synecdoche** /sɪˈnɛkdəki/ (Greek συνεκδοχή, "understanding together")
Part for whole or whole for part.
"All hands on deck." (hands = sailors)

**personification/prosopopoeia** /prəˌsɒpəˈpiːə/ (Greek προσωποποιΐα, "making a face")
Attribution of human qualities to non-human entities.
"Death, be not proud."

**apostrophe** /əˈpɒstrəfi/ (Greek ἀποστροφή, "turning away")
Direct address to an absent person, abstraction, or thing.
"O Death, where is thy sting?"

### Amplification & Diminution

**hyperbole** /haɪˈpɜːrbəli/ (Greek ὑπερβολή, "excess")
Deliberate exaggeration for emphasis.
"I've told you a million times."

**litotes** /ˈlaɪtətiːz/ (Greek λιτότης, "plainness")
Understatement by negating the contrary.
"He's not unintelligent." (= quite intelligent)

**meiosis** /maɪˈoʊsɪs/ (Greek μείωσις, "lessening")
Understatement; belittling for rhetorical effect.
"The Battle of Britain? Just a little dust-up."

**pleonasm** /ˈpliːənæzəm/ (Greek πλεονασμός, "excess")
Deliberate redundancy for emphasis.
"I saw it with my own eyes."

### Omission & Compression

**ellipsis** /ɪˈlɪpsɪs/ (Greek ἔλλειψις, "falling short")
Omission of words inferrable from context.
"Some rise by sin, and some [fall] by virtue."

**zeugma** /ˈzjuːɡmə/ (Greek ζεῦγμα, "yoking")
One word (often a verb) governing two or more unlike elements.
"He lost his coat and his temper."

**syllepsis** /sɪˈlɛpsɪs/ (Greek σύλληψις, "taking together")
Zeugma with semantic shift in the governing word.
"She lowered her standards and her neckline."

**asyndeton** /əˈsɪndɪtɒn/ (Greek ἀσύνδετον, "unconnected")
Omission of conjunctions between parallel elements.
"I came, I saw, I conquered."

**polysyndeton** /ˌpɒlɪˈsɪndɪtɒn/ (Greek πολυσύνδετον, "bound together")
Excessive use of conjunctions.
"And the rain descended, and the floods came, and the winds blew."

**brachylogia** /ˌbrækɪˈloʊdʒiə/ (Greek βραχυλογία, "brief speech")
Extreme compression or terseness.
"Came. Saw. Conquered."

### Sound Devices

**alliteration** (Latin ad- + littera, "to the letter")
Repetition of initial consonant sounds.
"Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers."

**assonance** (Latin assonare, "to sound to")
Repetition of vowel sounds.
"The rain in Spain falls mainly on the plain."

**consonance** (Latin consonare, "to sound together")
Repetition of consonant sounds, especially at word ends.
"The lumps and bumps of life."

**homoioteleuton** /ˌhɒmɔɪəʊtɪˈljuːtən/ (Greek ὁμοιοτέλευτον, "similar ending")
Words ending with similar sounds (not necessarily rhyme).
"He came to America penniless, friendless, powerless."

### Argument & Address

**erotema/rhetorical question** /ˌɛrəˈtiːmə/ (Greek ἐρώτημα, "question")
Question not expecting an answer, implying its own response.
"If not now, when?"

**hypophora** /haɪˈpɒfərə/ (Greek ὑποφορά, "carrying under")
Question posed and immediately answered by the speaker.
"What is the answer? There is no answer."

**aporia** /əˈpɔːriə/ (Greek ἀπορία, "difficulty")
Feigned doubt or deliberation about how to proceed.
"I hardly know where to begin."

**praeteritio/paralipsis** /ˌpærəˈlɪpsɪs/ (Greek παράλειψις, "passing over")
Drawing attention to something by claiming to pass over it.
"I will not mention his many bankruptcies."

**enthymeme** /ˈɛnθɪmiːm/ (Greek ἐνθύμημα, "consideration")
Syllogism with an implied premise.
"She's a politician, so she can't be trusted." (implied: all politicians are untrustworthy)

## Syntactic Analysis (Tufte Framework)

### Sentence Types

**periodic sentence**: Main clause delayed until the end; creates suspense.
"Despite the rain, the wind, and the utter lack of public interest, the parade went on."

**cumulative/loose sentence**: Main clause first, followed by modifying elements.
"The parade went on, despite the rain, the wind, and the utter lack of public interest."

**balanced sentence**: Two or more parallel independent clauses.
"The world will little note, nor long remember, what we say here, but it can never forget what they did here."

**freight-train sentence**: Series of coordinate clauses, often with repeated conjunctions.
"The snow fell and the wind howled and the night grew darker."

### Modification

**left-branching**: Modifiers precede the head.
"Exhausted, hungry, and disoriented, the hikers found camp."

**right-branching**: Modifiers follow the head.
"The hikers found camp, exhausted, hungry, and disoriented."

**mid-branching/embedding**: Modifiers interrupt the main clause.
"The hikers, exhausted and hungry, found camp."

**absolute phrase**: Noun + participle; grammatically independent of main clause.
"His eyes blazing with fury, he entered the room."

**appositive**: Noun phrase that renames or elaborates another.
"Shakespeare, the Bard of Avon, wrote 37 plays."

### Coordination & Subordination

**parataxis** /ˌpærəˈtæksɪs/ (Greek παράταξις, "placing beside")
Coordination without explicit subordination; equal clauses.
"The sun rose. The birds sang. The world awoke."

**hypotaxis** /haɪˈpɒtæksɪs/ (Greek ὑπόταξις, "arrangement under")
Subordination; hierarchical clause relationships.
"When the sun rose, which brightened the eastern sky, the birds that had been silent began to sing."
