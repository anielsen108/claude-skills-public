Create an Engagement Guide for the document: $ARGUMENTS

This guide is intended as a standalone alternative to reading the full text — not a summary, but a re-presentation that allows readers to engage with the author's ideas on their own terms.

If --community and/or --tradition flags are provided, use those values. Otherwise, infer the most appropriate audience and comparative tradition from the content of the document. If neither can be inferred, write the guide for a general intellectually engaged audience and omit the comparative connections section.

## Structure

### 1. Introduction
Write a brief framing statement that:
- Names this as an alternative to reading the full text, not a replacement
- Acknowledges what is lost by not reading the original (honoring the author's own thesis about how their ideas work)
- Identifies who this guide is for and why this format may serve them better

### 2. How to Use This Document
A short orientation (2-3 sentences) explaining the sections and how to navigate them.

### 3. About the Author
A short section (one paragraph) that:
- States the author's institutional position and specialization
- Names their approach and what makes it distinctive within their field
- Positions them honestly relative to mainstream scholarship (e.g., heterodox, minority view, novel synthesis)
- Tells the reader what kind of reading they're getting

### 4. The Central Argument
Present the book's overarching thesis in layers:
- Open with a vivid, accessible analogy that captures the core insight
- State the thesis in one clear sentence
- Provide 3-5 "In practice" bullet points showing what the thesis looks like concretely
- Distill to a single "if you take only one thing" sentence

### 5. What the Author Pushes Back Against
3-5 bullet points identifying the positions, assumptions, or frameworks the author is arguing against. For each:
- Name the position clearly
- Explain why the author rejects it (in 1-2 sentences)

### 6. Chapter Guide
For EACH chapter (including preface/introduction if substantive), provide:

**The Argument** — The central claim of this chapter in 2-4 sentences. State it directly, not as "the author argues that..." but as the claim itself.

**Key Concepts** — Bulleted list of the major concepts introduced or developed in this chapter. For each:
- Give the term (with original language/transliteration if relevant)
- Provide a concise definition (1-2 sentences)
- Where possible, include a concrete comparison or contrast

**Key Stories** — The most important illustrative stories, examples, or case studies from the chapter. For each:
- Include page references
- Retell the story briefly but completely enough to discuss
- Highlight the author's specific interpretation (which may differ from the conventional reading)

**Why This Matters to the Author** — 2-3 sentences connecting this chapter's argument to the book's overall thesis. Explain why this particular point is load-bearing for the author's project.

**Discussion Questions** — 2-4 questions that:
- Are experiential and personal, not academic ("What is X to you?" not "Define X")
- Invite the reader to test the author's claims against their own lived experience
- Where appropriate, connect to the reader community's own frameworks, practices, or vocabulary
- Occasionally provoke by juxtaposing the author's framework with the reader's tradition

### 7. Connections to Reader's Tradition/Framework
(Include this section only if a community/tradition is specified or can be clearly inferred.)

A dedicated section identifying resonances and distinctions between the book's themes and the reader community's own tradition. For each connection:
- Name the resonance clearly
- Note where the parallel holds and where it breaks down
- Use the community's own vocabulary and reference points
- Favor instructive distinctions over easy equivalences

### 8. Key Concepts Glossary
An alphabetized or thematically organized glossary of all major terms from the book. For each:
- Give the term with original language where relevant
- Define it in 2-3 sentences
- Include a concrete image, analogy, or contrast to make the definition sticky
- Note common misunderstandings where applicable (e.g., "NOT passivity or inaction")

## Tone and Style Guidelines
- Write with intellectual confidence. State claims directly rather than hedging.
- Use "you" — address the reader as a practitioner, not a student.
- Be honest about the author's position within their field (including when it's heterodox or contested).
- Favor bold, clear restatement over cautious academic paraphrase.
- Use vivid analogies (jazz, martial arts, cooking — whatever fits) to make abstract concepts tangible.
- Discussion questions should feel like prompts from a skilled facilitator, not exam questions.
- Include page references for key stories and claims so readers can go deeper.
- The guide should be self-sufficient: someone who reads only this guide should be able to participate meaningfully in a discussion of the book.

## Output
Save the completed engagement guide as a new file in the same directory as the source document, named: "[Document Title] — Engagement Guide.md"
